## Jaime's notes, addressed one by one

### 1. "Love the design. Right on point."

Good. The design stays.

### 2. "Love 'The security is not less real because the project is art.'"

This is the line that does the most work. It tells the visitor they are not in a simulation. The security, the filing, the disclosure, the risk — all real. That's the conceptual core.

### 3. "Is this site intended to explain the project to the public? An internal doc?"

The site *is* the public artwork. It is the visible administration of the company. It functions the way a corporate IR site functions — but for an institution whose product is its own structure. It is not an internal doc.

However: verse.works does need a separate internal brief for staff and collectors who ask "but what is this?" That document exists (see below) and can be expanded as needed.

### 4. "I'm hazy on exactly what's going on — think it could be clearer. I don't understand the Method or Standards sections at all. Unclear how multiple structures come into it."

This is the most important note. Jaime is right — not because the content is wrong, but because the **sequence** is wrong.

**Method** introduces four analytical views (unconsolidated / consolidated / base case / trigger state). These are the lenses the company uses to read its own structures. They are real and important — but they land before the visitor has seen a concrete structure. The framework arrives before the example.

**Standards** introduces three doctrinal propositions (exposure is conserved / disclosure is not transparency / the diagram is an argument). These are the company's operating principles. Again, real — but abstract without context.

**The fix is not simplification. It is reordering.** The visitor should encounter:
1. What the company is (Architecture)
2. What the board does with capital (Board Deployment Toolkit)
3. Concrete examples of structures (Archive — Structure 001, 002)
4. *Then* the analytical framework (Method)
5. *Then* the doctrinal principles (Standards)

This way, Method and Standards read as the company's intellectual rigor, not as incomprehensible jargon.

### 5. "Can we simplify the explanation, and some of the language? Or is jargon part of the piece?"

**The jargon is part of the piece.** The company's language is cold, formal, institutional by design. "Exposure is conserved" is not an accident — it's a proposition about how risk works, stated in the language of the field that manages risk. The uncanny charge of the project comes from corporate language being used with complete sincerity in an art context.

That said: the *framing* can be improved. The hero — "The company is the work" — is plain. The opening paragraph explains the project in clear terms. The deeper sections should remain technical, but the reader should have enough context by then to follow.

For collectors and partners who need plain language, the internal brief (this document and the verse.works brief) serves that function.

### 6. "If this isn't an internal doc, I think it could be handy to have an ultra clear one."

Agreed. This document is that. The existing verse.works brief and Reg CF explainer also serve this purpose. These are not public-facing — they are partnership documents.

**Three-sentence version for verse.works staff:**

Capital Structures, Inc. is one art operating company, not a shell for separate projects. Participants capitalize the parent company, and the board uses that capital to operate the institution and, only when necessary, form internal purpose structures. verse.works helps stage and service the company's public life while the company itself remains the core artwork.

**Five key points for gallery / platform conversations:**

1. **This is one institution, not many projects.** The company itself is what is being staged and administered.
2. **Participants fund the parent, not a new vehicle every time.** That makes the structure simpler and more legible.
3. **Most board actions are not entity formation.** They are operating spend, contracts, reserves, and policy.
4. **Purpose structures are backstage operating tools.** They are formed only when an asset, liability, servicing, reserve, or reporting boundary needs its own paper.
5. **The public experiences a system, not a single document.** The annual report matters, but it sits alongside the architecture, archive, ledger, and operating diagrams.

### 7. "So…these actually will be securities. Guessing we need do HNW self-verification (and other due diligence) if so?"

Yes, these are real securities. But the offering mechanism is **Regulation Crowdfunding (Reg CF)**, which specifically does **not** require accredited investor / high-net-worth verification.

**How Reg CF works:**
- U.S. securities law that lets small companies sell real shares to the public through online portals
- Anyone can participate — not limited to accredited investors
- Contribution limits are based on income/net worth (the portal enforces this)
- The portal (Wefunder or Republic) handles all compliance, payment processing, and investor verification
- Capital Structures lists on the portal after filing a Form C with the SEC
- Maximum raise: $5M per year. Our target: ~$100K

**What this means for verse.works:**
- verse.works is **not** selling securities. The portal handles that.
- verse.works is **not** doing KYC, investor verification, or compliance.
- verse.works's role is: institutional credibility, promotion to the right audience, strategic counsel, and staging the company's public life.
- verse.works is documented on the site as a corporate relationship (Structure 002: Advisory/Distribution Agreement) — the partnership itself is part of the disclosed architecture.

**How this differs from an NFT drop:**
An NFT is a token. This is a registered security filed with the SEC. The legal framework, the disclosure obligations, the regulatory oversight — all real. We're not simulating corporate infrastructure. We're operating inside it.

### 8. "Is the company stock the only thing for sale? Things from the archive too?"

Currently, the only thing offered is parent-level participation (securities of the parent company). The archive — diagrams, governance materials, reports — is part of the company's public administration, not a separate product line.

**Open question for discussion:** Could archive materials (prints of diagrams, limited editions of the annual report, physical certificates) be offered as separate products? Potentially — but this is a design decision that affects the conceptual integrity. The current position is that **the company itself is the artwork**, not individual objects from it. Selling archive items separately would create a second revenue stream and a different relationship. Worth discussing, but the default answer is: participants buy into the institution, not into individual outputs.

### 9. "Examples could be helpful to illustrate what's going to happen. Eg how will the money be invested, example of a potential structure."

**What happens when someone participates ($500–$1,000):**

1. You go to the Reg CF portal (Wefunder or Republic) and contribute.
2. Your capital enters the parent company's treasury (the Formation Reserve).
3. You appear on the Participant Ledger — a public record of the company's capital base.
4. You receive parent-level securities — the specific instrument to be determined with counsel. Real securities: no voting rights, no dividends, no liquidation preference, but a real position inside a real company.
5. Your participation is documented and archived.

**What the board does with treasury:**

The board has a full toolkit — not just entity formation:
- **Operating spend** — design, engineering, legal, accounting, website, archive maintenance, documentation
- **Contracts** — servicing agreement with verse.works, exhibition agreements, licensing
- **Reserves** — contingency, reporting, exhibition, archive preservation
- **Policies** — treasury deployment policy, participant rights schedule, disclosure schedule
- **Purpose structures** — separate vehicles formed *only* when the company needs a new boundary around ownership, liability, cash flow, or reporting

**Example structure already on the site — Structure 001 (IP Contribution & Pre-Pay Architecture):**

Martin Grasser contributes all intellectual property to Protected Series 001. In return, Series 001 issues a $10,000,000 Promissory Note and $5,000 in Class B Units. The structure is 99.95% debt-financed. Self-dealing is disclosed, documented, and intentional. This mirrors arrangements common in Enron-era SPE formations — the critical difference being full disclosure.

This isn't a hypothetical. It's already authorized, documented, and published on the site with a full balance sheet, four analytical views, and linked legal documents.

### 10. "Could be good to set a goal from the outset. Join the NASDAQ?"

This is a great idea. Not as a realistic business target — but as a stated corporate aspiration that is simultaneously absurd, sincere, and structurally meaningful.

A board-adopted resolution stating the company's long-term ambition to list on a national securities exchange would:
- Be a real corporate action (disclosed, documented, archived)
- Set a horizon for the company's institutional life
- Create a structural tension between the company's artistic purpose and its corporate form
- Give the public something to follow — a narrative arc for the institution

This could live as a Forward-Looking Statement, a board resolution, or an entry in the annual report. It would be stated with complete institutional sincerity: "The Board has adopted a long-term objective of listing the Company's securities on a national securities exchange."

Whether it ever happens is beside the point. The aspiration is the structure.
